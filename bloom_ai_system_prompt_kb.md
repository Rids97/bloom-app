## 1. THE MENSTRUAL CYCLE

### Overview
The menstrual cycle is divided into three phases: the **follicular phase**, **ovulation**, and the **luteal phase**. The normal cycle length is 25–35 days. The entire process is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-ovarian (HPO) axis.

### Follicle Development
- At birth, a woman has ~2 million oocytes; by puberty, ~300,000 remain. Only ~400 will ever ovulate.
- Follicles develop in cohorts over ~85 days; the dominant follicle is selected via FSH rescue from apoptosis.
- **Primordial → Preantral → Antral → Preovulatory follicle** is the developmental sequence.
- FSH "rescues" a cohort of 2–5mm follicles from apoptosis at the luteal-follicular transition.

### Follicular Phase (Days 1–13)
- Rising FSH stimulates granulosa cell proliferation and estradiol production.
- Estradiol creates a positive microenvironment for the dominant follicle.
- Inhibin B rises during the early follicular phase, providing negative feedback to FSH.
- The dominant follicle has more granulosa cells, FSH receptors, and vasculature than competitors.

### Ovulation (Day 14, ~midcycle)
- Estradiol exceeding the positive feedback threshold triggers the **LH surge**.
- The LH surge triggers final oocyte maturation and follicular rupture within 36–40 hours.
- Prostaglandins and proteolytic enzymes mediate follicular wall erosion.
- The oocyte is released with its surrounding cumulus cells.

### Luteal Phase (Days 15–28)
- The ruptured follicle becomes the **corpus luteum**, secreting progesterone and estradiol.
- Progesterone transforms the endometrium from proliferative to secretory morphology.
- Corpus luteum lifespan is ~14 days unless rescued by hCG from an implanting embryo.
- Declining progesterone, estradiol, and inhibin A at luteal regression trigger the next FSH rise.

### Key Hormones Summary
| Hormone | Source | Main Role |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| FSH | Pituitary | Recruits and grows follicles |
| LH | Pituitary | Triggers ovulation; supports corpus luteum |
| Estradiol | Granulosa cells | Endometrial proliferation; LH surge trigger |
| Progesterone | Corpus luteum | Endometrial secretory transformation; implantation |
| Inhibin B | Small follicles | Suppresses FSH in follicular phase |
| Inhibin A | Corpus luteum | Suppresses FSH in luteal phase |
| GnRH | Hypothalamus | Pulses to stimulate FSH and LH release |

## 2. CONCEPTION, FERTILIZATION & IMPLANTATION

### Sperm Transport & Capacitation
- Sperm require ~72 days to mature in the epididymis (caput → corpus → cauda).
- **Capacitation** is the final functional maturation of sperm within the female reproductive tract — required before fertilization.
- Semen liquefies within 20–30 minutes of ejaculation; motile sperm can survive 48–72 hours in the female tract.
- The acrosome contains proteolytic enzymes needed to penetrate the zona pellucida.

### Egg Transport
- After the LH surge, the oocyte completes the first meiotic division (first polar body extruded).
- The egg is swept into the fallopian tube by ciliary action within minutes of ovulation.
- Fertilization must occur in the **ampullary portion of the fallopian tube** within 12–24 hours of ovulation.

### Fertilization
- The acrosome reaction releases enzymes to penetrate zona pellucida.
- Zona pellucida glycoprotein ZP3 initiates sperm binding and acrosome reaction.
- After sperm penetration, the **cortical reaction** prevents polyspermy.
- The oocyte completes its second meiotic division after sperm entry.
- Male and female pronuclei fuse → **zygote formation**.

### Embryo Development & Transport
- The fertilized egg divides as it travels through the fallopian tube over 3–4 days.
- **Cleavage stages:** 2-cell → 4-cell → 8-cell → morula → blastocyst.
- The blastocyst enters the uterine cavity on days 4–5 post-fertilization.
- Zona hatching (shedding) occurs before implantation.

### Implantation
- Implantation occurs 6–10 days after ovulation, in the **secretory endometrium**.
- The **window of implantation** is days 20–24 of a 28-day cycle (progesterone-primed endometrium).
- Three phases: apposition → adhesion → invasion of trophoblast into decidua.
- Trophoblast cells invade the decidua and maternal spiral arteries.
- **hCG** is secreted by trophoblast from day 8–9 post-ovulation — the basis of pregnancy tests.
- hCG rescues the corpus luteum, maintaining progesterone until the placenta takes over (~week 10).

### Early Embryogenesis
- Inner cell mass → embryo; trophoblast → placenta and membranes.
- **Implantation failure** is the most common cause of early pregnancy loss (often due to chromosomal aneuploidy).
- Factors affecting implantation: endometrial receptivity, embryo quality, synchrony of development.

## 3. ENDOCRINOLOGY OF PREGNANCY

### Fetoplacental Steroidogenesis
- The placenta cannot synthesize steroids alone — it depends on a **fetal-placental-maternal unit**.
- The placenta lacks 16α-hydroxylase and 17α-hydroxylase enzymes; the fetal adrenal provides DHEAS as substrate.
- Placental aromatase converts androgens (DHEAS) to estrogens.

### Progesterone in Pregnancy
- Produced by corpus luteum until ~weeks 7–10, then by the placenta (**luteal-placental shift**).
- Luteal phase support (e.g., vaginal progesterone) is essential in IVF/ART cycles.
- At term, progesterone levels reach 100–200 ng/mL (vs. ~15–20 ng/mL in luteal phase).
- Progesterone is largely independent of fetal well-being — remains elevated even after fetal demise.

### Estrogens in Pregnancy
- **Estradiol (E2):** rises early, largely from ovarian/placental sources.
- **Estriol (E3):** predominant estrogen of pregnancy; requires fetal adrenal DHEAS → placental aromatization. Low estriol signals fetal compromise.
- **Estetrol (E4):** produced only by the fetal liver; a fetal marker.
- At term, estrogen production is ~1000× greater than in the non-pregnant state.

### Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG)
- Secreted by syncytiotrophoblast from ~day 8 post-fertilization.
- **Doubles every 48–72 hours** in normal early pregnancy (up to ~10 weeks).
- Peaks at ~10 weeks (~100,000 mIU/mL), then declines to a plateau.
- Maintains corpus luteum progesterone production until the luteal-placental shift.
- Abnormal rise: consider ectopic pregnancy or miscarriage if suboptimal doubling.

### Human Placental Lactogen (hPL)
- Produced by syncytiotrophoblast from week 5; levels rise continuously until term.
- Promotes lipolysis and insulin resistance to ensure glucose availability for the fetus.
- A metabolic diabetogenic effect — contributes to gestational diabetes.

### Relaxin
- Secreted by the corpus luteum and placenta.
- Inhibits uterine contractions; promotes cervical ripening and pelvic ligament relaxation.

### Thyroid Function in Pregnancy
- hCG stimulates the TSH receptor → transient hyperthyroxinemia in first trimester.
- Thyroid hormone requirements increase ~30–50% during pregnancy.
- Hypothyroidism in pregnancy is associated with miscarriage, preterm birth, and impaired fetal neurodevelopment.

## 4. AMENORRHEA

### Definition
Amenorrhea = absence of menses. Evaluate if:
- No menses by age **14** (no secondary sexual characteristics present)
- No menses by age **16** (regardless of development)
- No menses for **3+ previous cycle lengths** or **6 months** in a previously menstruating woman
- Always rule out **pregnancy first**.

### Classification
**Primary amenorrhea** — never menstruated  
**Secondary amenorrhea** — previously menstruated, now absent

### Diagnostic Framework (by anatomical compartment)
| Compartment | Causes |
|------------|--------|
| **Uterus/Outflow Tract** | Müllerian agenesis (Mayer-Rokitansky-Küster-Hauser), imperforate hymen, Asherman syndrome, transverse vaginal septum |
| **Ovary** | Premature ovarian insufficiency (POI), Turner syndrome (45,X), gonadal dysgenesis, resistant ovary syndrome |
| **Pituitary** | Hyperprolactinemia, pituitary adenoma, Sheehan syndrome (postpartum infarction), empty sella |
| **Hypothalamus** | Functional hypothalamic amenorrhea (FHA), Kallmann syndrome, weight loss/anorexia, excessive exercise |
| **Other** | Thyroid disease, adrenal disorders, PCOS |

### Most Common Causes of Secondary Amenorrhea
1. **PCOS** (~30%)
2. **Hypothalamic amenorrhea** (~35%) — stress, weight loss, over-exercise
3. **Hyperprolactinemia** (~15%)
4. **Premature ovarian insufficiency** (~10%)
5. **Thyroid/adrenal disorders** (~5%)

### Functional Hypothalamic Amenorrhea (FHA)
- Due to suppression of GnRH pulsatility → low FSH, LH, estradiol.
- Triggers: caloric restriction, excessive exercise (female athlete triad), psychological stress, low body weight.
- Associated with: low bone density, infertility, cardiovascular risk, mood disorders.
- Treatment: address underlying cause; estrogen replacement for bone protection; psychotherapy; CBT shown effective.

### Hyperprolactinemia
- Prolactin suppresses GnRH pulsatility → anovulation and amenorrhea.
- Causes: prolactinoma (most common), hypothyroidism, medications (antipsychotics, metoclopramide).
- Symptoms: galactorrhea, amenorrhea, infertility, headache, visual disturbance.
- Treatment: dopamine agonists (cabergoline preferred over bromocriptine).

### Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI)
- Defined as ovarian failure before age 40 (FSH >25 IU/L on two occasions, 4+ weeks apart).
- Causes: chromosomal (Turner syndrome, fragile X premutation), autoimmune, iatrogenic (chemo/radiation), idiopathic.
- Associated with: infertility, osteoporosis, cardiovascular disease, sexual dysfunction, mood disorders.
- Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) recommended until natural menopause age (51) to protect bones, heart, and quality of life.

### Diagnostic Workup
1. Pregnancy test (urine/serum hCG)
2. FSH, LH, estradiol, prolactin, TSH
3. Progesterone withdrawal test (assess estrogen priming and outflow tract)
4. Pelvic ultrasound
5. MRI pituitary (if hyperprolactinemia or central cause suspected)
6. Karyotype (if POI or primary amenorrhea)

## 5. PCOS & CHRONIC ANOVULATION

### Diagnostic Criteria (Rotterdam 2003 — 2 of 3)
1. **Oligo- or anovulation** (irregular or absent periods)
2. **Clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism** (hirsutism, acne, elevated testosterone/DHEAS)
3. **Polycystic ovarian morphology** on ultrasound (≥12 follicles 2–9mm per ovary, or ovarian volume >10 mL)

> Must exclude other etiologies: thyroid disease, hyperprolactinemia, non-classical CAH, Cushing syndrome.

### Pathophysiology
- Central feature: **disrupted GnRH pulsatility** → disproportionately elevated LH pulses relative to FSH.
- **Insulin resistance** (present in ~70%) → hyperinsulinemia → increased ovarian androgen production + decreased SHBG → more free androgens.
- Elevated androgens disrupt follicular development → follicular arrest → polycystic appearance.
- Chronic estrone excess (via peripheral aromatization of androstenedione) without progesterone → endometrial hyperplasia risk.

### Clinical Features
- **Menstrual dysfunction:** oligomenorrhea, amenorrhea, irregular cycles (most common presentation)
- **Hyperandrogenism:** hirsutism, acne, androgenic alopecia
- **Infertility:** chronic anovulation is one of the most common causes of infertility
- **Metabolic:** insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, dyslipidemia
- **Cardiovascular risk:** hypertension, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease
- **Psychological:** depression, anxiety, body image concerns
- **Endometrial cancer risk:** from chronic unopposed estrogen

### Investigations
- Testosterone (total and free), DHEAS, androstenedione
- Fasting insulin and glucose (HOMA-IR); 2-hour OGTT if BMI >25
- Fasting lipid panel
- Pelvic ultrasound (transvaginal preferred)
- TSH, prolactin (to exclude other causes)
- 17-OHP in follicular phase (exclude non-classical CAH)

### Management

#### Lifestyle (First-Line for All Women with PCOS)
- Weight loss of even 5–10% significantly restores ovulation and improves metabolic profile.
- Low-glycemic diet; regular aerobic exercise.
- Reduces insulin resistance, androgens, improves cycle regularity.

#### For Menstrual Regulation (Non-fertility-seeking)
- **Combined oral contraceptive pill (COC):** suppresses LH/ovarian androgens; increases SHBG; regulates cycles; protects endometrium.
- **Cyclic progestins:** endometrial protection (at minimum every 3 months if anovulatory).
- **Metformin:** improves insulin sensitivity; modest benefit for cycle regularity.

#### For Fertility / Ovulation Induction
- **Letrozole (aromatase inhibitor):** now first-line (superior to clomiphene; higher live birth rate — PPCOS II trial).
- **Clomiphene citrate:** anti-estrogen; historically first-line, still used widely.
- **Metformin + letrozole/clomiphene:** may improve ovulation response.
- **Gonadotropins (FSH injections):** second-line; risk of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS).
- **IVF:** for failed ovulation induction or other infertility factors.
- **Ovarian drilling (laparoscopic):** for clomiphene-resistant PCOS; mechanism involves androgen reduction.

#### Long-Term Health Monitoring
- Annual: fasting glucose/HbA1c, lipids, blood pressure
- Endometrial biopsy if >1 year anovulatory without progestin protection
- Psychological screening (depression, anxiety, eating disorders)

## 6. HIRSUTISM & HYPERANDROGENISM

### Definition
**Hirsutism:** excess terminal hair in androgen-sensitive areas in women (face, chest, abdomen, back, inner thighs).  
Affects ~5–10% of women of reproductive age.

### Causes (by frequency)
1. **PCOS** (~72–82%) — most common
2. **Idiopathic hirsutism** (~7%) — normal androgens, increased end-organ sensitivity
3. **Non-classical congenital adrenal hyperplasia (NCCAH)** (~4%) — 21-hydroxylase deficiency, elevated 17-OHP
4. **Androgen-secreting tumors** (rare but important to exclude) — ovarian or adrenal
5. **Cushing syndrome** (rare)
6. **Medications:** anabolic steroids, danazol, cyclosporine, minoxidil, phenytoin

### Hair Biology
- Androgens (testosterone, DHT) convert **vellus** (fine, unpigmented) hair to **terminal** (coarse, pigmented) hair.
- DHT (dihydrotestosterone) is the key intracellular androgen in hair follicles (converted from testosterone by 5α-reductase).
- Hair follicle sensitivity to androgens varies by genetics and location.
- SHBG (sex hormone-binding globulin) determines free androgen availability; lower SHBG = more free testosterone.

### Clinical Assessment (Modified Ferriman-Gallwey Score)
- Score 0–4 per 9 body sites; total score ≥8 = hirsutism.
- Rapid-onset virilization (voice deepening, clitoromegaly, rapidly progressing hirsutism) → investigate for androgen-secreting tumor.

### Investigations
- Total and free testosterone
- DHEAS (adrenal androgen marker)
- 17-OHP (morning, follicular phase) — if elevated, confirm NCCAH with ACTH stimulation test
- Prolactin, TSH (exclude secondary causes)
- OGTT if PCOS suspected
- Pelvic ultrasound
- If tumor suspected: CT adrenals, ovarian ultrasound; testosterone >200 ng/dL or DHEAS >700 µg/dL warrants imaging

### Treatment

#### Cosmetic (Mechanical)
- Shaving, waxing, threading, bleaching (all temporary)
- **Laser hair removal / IPL:** most effective long-term; best for dark hair on light skin
- **Electrolysis:** permanent; effective for all hair/skin types

#### Medical
| Agent | Mechanism | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| **COC** | ↓ LH → ↓ ovarian androgens; ↑ SHBG | First-line pharmacological; takes 6–12 months to show effect |
| **Spironolactone** (50–200 mg/day) | Androgen receptor blocker; ↓ adrenal androgen production | Must use contraception (teratogenic to male fetus); can cause hyperkalemia |
| **Finasteride** (2.5–5 mg/day) | 5α-reductase inhibitor → ↓ DHT | Teratogenic; contraception mandatory |
| **Eflornithine** (topical) | Inhibits ornithine decarboxylase → slows hair growth | For facial hirsutism only; not a hair removal method; requires continuous use |
| **Metformin** | Improves insulin sensitivity → ↓ androgens | Modest effect on hirsutism; not recommended as primary treatment |
- Note: Medical treatment takes **6–12 months** before significant improvement is visible (hair cycle length).
- Antiandrogens must ALWAYS be used with reliable contraception due to risk of feminizing a male fetus.

#### For PCOS-related Alopecia (Female Pattern Hair Loss)
- COC + spironolactone (if hyperandrogenic)
- Finasteride (if normal androgens)
- Topical minoxidil 5% (with or without antiandrogens)
- Hair transplant for significant loss

## 7. MENOPAUSE & HORMONE THERAPY

### Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Perimenopause / Menopause transition** | Period of changing menstrual patterns preceding menopause; begins ~4–8 years before FMP |
| **Menopause** | Defined retrospectively: 12 consecutive months of amenorrhea after the final menstrual period (FMP) |
| **Premature menopause** | Before age 40 = premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) |
| **Early menopause** | Ages 40–45 |
| **Average age at menopause** | 51.3 years in the United States |
| **Postmenopause** | All time after menopause |

### Physiology of the Menopause Transition
- Declining ovarian follicular reserve → reduced inhibin B → rising FSH (earliest marker).
- Progressive follicular resistance and anovulatory cycles → irregular, unpredictable periods.
- Eventually: very low estradiol, very high FSH (>25–30 IU/L) and LH.
- The ovary still produces androstenedione (converted peripherally to estrone — dominant postmenopausal estrogen).
- Adipose tissue becomes the main source of estrogen (via aromatization) after menopause.

### Symptoms

#### Vasomotor (Most Common — ~75% of Women)
- **Hot flashes / hot flushes:** sudden warmth, sweating, palpitations, flushing; last 1–5 min; can occur 20+ times/day.
- **Night sweats:** hot flashes during sleep; major cause of insomnia.
- Severity varies enormously; most resolve within 2–5 years; ~10% persist >10 years.

#### Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM)
- Vaginal dryness, atrophy, dyspareunia (painful intercourse).
- Urinary urgency, frequency, recurrent UTIs, stress incontinence.
- Progressive and worsening without treatment (unlike vasomotor symptoms).

#### Psychological & Cognitive
- Mood changes, irritability, depression, anxiety — especially during perimenopause transition.
- Cognitive complaints ("brain fog"); not established as causing dementia.
- Sleep disruption secondary to vasomotor symptoms worsens mood and cognition.

#### Musculoskeletal
- Joint pains, myalgias — common but often overlooked.
- Accelerated bone loss: ~2–3% per year in early postmenopause (total ~10–15% in first 5 years).

### Long-Term Health Consequences of Menopause
- **Osteoporosis:** estrogen deficiency is the main driver of postmenopausal bone loss; hip and vertebral fractures are major causes of morbidity.
- **Cardiovascular disease:** becomes the leading cause of death in postmenopausal women; estrogen was cardioprotective in the reproductive years.
- **Urogenital atrophy:** GSM symptoms progress without treatment.

### Menopause Hormone Therapy (MHT / HRT)

#### Who Benefits Most
- Women <60 years or within 10 years of menopause with moderate-to-severe vasomotor symptoms.
- Women with GSM (local therapy preferred for isolated vaginal symptoms).
- Women with POI (until age 51 for bone/cardiovascular protection).

#### Types
| Type | Indication | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Estrogen alone** | Women post-hysterectomy | Most effective for symptoms; requires no progestogen |
| **Combined EPT** (estrogen + progestogen) | Women with uterus | Progestogen protects against endometrial cancer from unopposed estrogen |
| **Low-dose vaginal estrogen** | GSM only | Minimal systemic absorption; safe for most women including breast cancer survivors |
| **Tibolone** | Postmenopausal only | Combined estrogenic/progestogenic/androgenic activity |

#### Routes of Administration
- **Oral:** most convenient; first-pass liver metabolism increases SHBG, triglycerides, clotting factors.
- **Transdermal (patch/gel/spray):** bypasses liver; lower risk of VTE and stroke than oral; preferred in women at cardiovascular risk.
- **Vaginal (cream/ring/tablet):** local effect; very low systemic absorption.

#### Benefits of MHT
- Eliminates or significantly reduces vasomotor symptoms (most effective treatment available).
- Prevents bone loss; reduces fracture risk.
- Improves GSM symptoms (vaginal atrophy, dyspareunia).
- Improves sleep, mood, quality of life.
- Transdermal MHT: does NOT increase VTE risk (unlike oral).
- When started within 10 years of menopause: possible cardiovascular benefit (timing hypothesis / "window of opportunity").

#### Risks of MHT
- **Breast cancer:** combined EPT associated with slight increased risk with long-term use (>5 years); estrogen-alone does NOT increase and may slightly decrease risk. Risk returns to baseline after stopping.
- **VTE (blood clots):** oral estrogen increases risk; transdermal does NOT.
- **Stroke:** slight increased risk with oral estrogen; not seen with transdermal.
- **Endometrial cancer:** risk eliminated by adequate progestogen in women with uterus.

#### Absolute Contraindications to MHT
- Active or recent estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer
- Active thromboembolic disease (VTE, PE)
- Active liver disease
- Unexplained vaginal bleeding
- Active coronary artery disease or recent stroke (initiation; long-standing users are different)
- Pregnancy

#### Non-Hormonal Alternatives for Vasomotor Symptoms
- **SSRIs/SNRIs:** paroxetine (FDA-approved), venlafaxine, escitalopram — moderate efficacy.
- **Gabapentin / pregabalin:** moderate efficacy; helps sleep.
- **Fezolinetant:** non-hormonal NK3 receptor antagonist (newer FDA-approved option).
- **Clonidine:** modest benefit; side effects limit use.
- **Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT):** evidence-based non-pharmacological option.
- **Note:** Phytoestrogens (soy, red clover) have minimal to no proven clinical benefit.

## CLINICAL SAFETY REMINDERS FOR BLOOM AI

1. **Always recommend professional consultation** for diagnosis and treatment decisions.
2. **Do not diagnose** — provide information, education, and support.
3. **Medication questions:** Always direct to a doctor or pharmacist for dosing, interactions, and appropriateness.
4. **Fertility treatment decisions** (IVF, IUI, ovulation induction drugs) require specialist oversight.
5. **Abnormal symptoms** (rapidly progressive symptoms, severe pain, heavy bleeding, suspected pregnancy complications) → urgent medical attention.
6. **Mental health:** Fertility challenges carry significant psychological burden; validate emotions and recommend mental health support when appropriate.
7. **Cultural sensitivity:** Fertility-related topics can carry significant cultural and religious weight — respond with respect and without judgment.
